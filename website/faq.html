Frequently Asked Questions

How often should I water my plants?

There's no one-size-fits-all answer, as watering needs vary by plant type, pot size, environmental conditions, and season. However, most houseplants prefer to dry out between waterings. Rather than following a strict schedule, check the soil moisture by inserting your finger about 2 inches deep. If it feels dry, it's time to water. For most common houseplants, this means watering every 1-2 weeks.

What does "bright indirect light" actually mean?

Bright indirect light means your plant receives plenty of light, but not direct sun rays hitting the leaves. Good spots for bright indirect light include:

  • Near east or west-facing windows
  • A few feet back from a south-facing window
  • Directly in front of a north-facing window (in the Northern Hemisphere)

If you can see a distinct shadow with soft edges when you hold your hand up, that's usually bright indirect light.

My plant has yellow leaves. What's wrong?

Yellowing leaves can be caused by several factors:

  • Overwatering - The most common cause, often accompanied by soft, mushy stems
  • Underwatering - Usually paired with crispy leaf edges and dry soil
  • Light issues - Too much or too little light
  • Nutrient deficiencies - Especially if you haven't fertilized in a long time
  • Natural aging - Old leaves naturally yellow and drop off

Check our detailed guide on why plant leaves turn yellow for more information and solutions.

I have pets. Which plants are safe?

Many common houseplants are toxic to pets if ingested. Safe options for pet owners include:

  • Spider Plant (Chlorophytum comosum)
  • Boston Fern (Nephrolepis exaltata)
  • Calathea varieties
  • Peperomia varieties
  • Air Plants (Tillandsia)
  • Most true Ferns
  • Prayer Plant (Maranta leuconeura)

Always verify a plant's safety before bringing it home if you have pets who might nibble on your plants.

How do I get rid of fungus gnats?

Fungus gnats are tiny flies that hover around houseplants, attracted to moist soil. To eliminate them:

  1. Let the soil dry out more between waterings
  2. Use sticky traps to catch adult flies
  3. Apply a hydrogen peroxide solution (1 part 3% hydrogen peroxide to 4 parts water) to the soil
  4. Top the soil with a layer of sand or diatomaceous earth
  5. Consider repotting with fresh, sterile potting mix

Do I need special soil for indoor plants?

Yes, using soil specifically formulated for indoor plants is recommended. Regular garden soil is too heavy and can lead to root rot. Indoor potting mixes provide better drainage, aeration, and are usually sterile (reducing pest issues). Some plants like succulents, orchids, and cacti require specialty mixes tailored to their specific needs.
